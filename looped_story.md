# LOOPED — Game Concept & Narrative Fusion (Beta Framework)

## 1. The Protagonist Shift: The 80-Year Loop
You are not a conscripted soldier or a reluctant mage; you are a wanderer who simply walked too far out of safety. You stumbled upon a **Time Sphere**—a glowing, anomalous relic of the Void. Touching it didn't just pull you in; it trapped you in a closed temporal loop for the last 80 years. 

This Time Sphere is now your permanent anchor. It is the literal Gate that teleports you into the dungeon for each run, and the tether that yanks you back when you die. You aren't fighting to save the world; you are fighting to finally break an 8-decade cycle. This temporal trap creates a deeply satisfying, logical world-building progression where the rules of the isolated world are a physical puzzle you must master through repetition.

## 2. Realigning "Erasure": The Arm Monitor as Containment
To reward the player for chasing god-tier builds, the concept of "Alignment" is preserved but reframed entirely around your hardware. 

The 7 Void Frequencies still want to consume reality. However, your **Arm Monitor** acts as a localized containment cell. Slotting passive cards doesn't corrupt your soul; it weaponizes a controlled leak of that cosmic frequency. Pushing your build to the absolute limit is the power fantasy of forcing the Void to work for you. "The Final Convergence" is simply what happens when the hardware fails. The final boss, "The Looped," is a reflection of your possible fate: a Hunter whose monitor shattered long ago, leaving him permanently merged with his own broken combo. And if a sharp-eyed player studies the shattered monitor fused into the boss's arm, they'll notice something: it carries a **maker's mark**. The same mark as the one on yours.

## 3. The Found Family: Single-Rescue Extraction
Instead of starting with a populated hub, you start almost alone with the Time Sphere — almost, because one figure is already there: **Orin the Loopwright**, the old monitor-maker who fits every new arrival with an Arm Monitor and gently shows them how to survive. Lysa, Brann, Serin, and Mira are lost somewhere within the dungeon.

During your runs, you will encounter them and must complete a one-time rescue event. Once extracted, they return to the hub area around the Time Sphere permanently. Each rescued character will grant you a specific, persistent buff to aid in future loops. Their presence mechanically transforms the hub from a lonely prison into a growing base camp without adding premature scope to the beta.

## 4. Pacing the Madness: Beta-Friendly Escalation
Since the game is currently in the beta phase, the development scope is kept highly manageable. Advanced, engine-breaking dynamic audio or shifting geometry are set aside for later stages. 

Instead, the "descent into madness" is handled purely through standard, achievable roguelike escalation:
* **Floor 1:** Standard lighting, clean enemy behaviors, and basic combat geometry.
* **Floor 2 & 3:** The environmental storytelling shifts using static assets and simpler variables. The lighting palette changes (e.g., turning sickly or high-contrast), enemy density spikes, and the architecture of the generated rooms utilizes more chaotic, pre-built prefabs.
* **The Reset:** The psychological weight comes entirely from the loop itself. Whether you clear the floor or get crushed, the Time Sphere violently resets you back to zero.

## 5. The Loopwright's Debt: Who Made the Loop (THE TWIST — spoilers)
The loop didn't happen to the world. It was **done** to the world — by the gentlest person in your hub.

Eighty years ago, **Orin the Loopwright** was the finest monitor-maker alive, and his masterpiece was the monitor he built for the first true Hunter — his friend, his life's proudest work. During a Final Convergence deep in the dungeon, that monitor shattered. Not from enemy action: from a flaw in Orin's containment lattice. The Hunter merged with his own broken combo and became the thing now called **The Looped**.

Facing a creature about to consume reality — a creature wearing his friend's face and his own maker's mark — Orin could not kill him. So he did something bigger than murder: he seized the **Time Sphere** and folded the world's last day into a closed loop. A cage made of repetition. The catastrophe never finishes happening, because the day it happens on never finishes either.

He has been paying interest on that decision ever since. Every wanderer the Sphere swallows wakes up to a kind old man who fits them with an Arm Monitor and shows them the ropes. The tutorial was never kindness — it is **recruitment**. Orin arms Hunter after Hunter, loop after loop, hoping one of them will finally do the thing he couldn't do himself.

**The game already proves the twist mechanically:** killing The Looped does not break the loop. The Time Sphere still resets you. That isn't a roguelike concession anymore — it is the reveal. The Hunter was never the loop's anchor. *The maker is.*

### How it recolors the cast (in hindsight)
* **Orin** hands you the monitor in the tutorial — exactly as he once handed one to The Looped.
* **Mira**, the frequency-reader, was scattered into the loop's hum — a hum that carries a workshop signature.
* **Vorr** lives "in the seams between resets" — the seams of *Orin's machine*. The Hollow Broker almost certainly knows, and profits either way.
* **The Time Sphere secret** (the hidden run-shortcut) works because the Sphere is the loop's beating heart — the Loopwright's actual masterpiece.

### Reveal cadence (drip-feed, cheapest first)
1. **Breadcrumbs:** maker's marks on monitor shards in the final arena match your own monitor; Orin's dialogue slips ("you always ask that"); curse events whisper of "the Loopwright's debt."
2. **Suspicion:** once the found family is home, Mira mentions the loop's hum sounds *built* — and that she's heard the builder's signature before, in the hub.
3. **Confession:** the first time you kill The Looped and the world resets anyway, Orin is waiting at the Time Sphere with the truth.
4. **The Debt (true ending, post-beta):** unmake the loop at its anchor — with or without forgiving the man who made it.
